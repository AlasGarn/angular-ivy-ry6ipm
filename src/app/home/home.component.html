ABOUT ME

I am fascinated by how life is based around complex structures and mechanisms that emerge from the collective motion of atoms. As a researcher, I try to solve biological problems to answer questions about the function of molecular machines and how diseases arise from their malfunction.

For the past eight years, I have used computational models, spanning the spectrum from quantum mechanics to coarse-grained molecular dynamics, with the help of Markov state models and enhanced sampling techniques to describe complex biomolecular systems in great detail. My work has taken me into a range of fascinating areas, including transcription with RNA polymerase II and antibody design, among others.

Currently, I am a post-doctoral researcher at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, working alongside Xuhui Huang. Through my work I hope to advance our understanding of the way life works and help people suffering from diseases.

It takes more than one hydrogen bond to stop a transcribing RNA polymerase II

The RNA polymerase II transcribes almost all protein coding genes in eukaryotes and is very sensitive to modifications of the DNA. By using Markov state models, I found that 5-carboxylcytosine - an epigenetic modification of cytosine - can stop the Pol II by interfering with its internal machinery in suprizingly many ways.
